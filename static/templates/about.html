The Prodigal About Page

The TL;DR Version.

Bitbanter lets you submit original, creative articles (even anonymously) that others can tip through bitcoins (yes, even anonymously). The higher the tips, the more prominent your article. 80% of your tips are transferred to you; the other 20% keeps me incentivized to keep Bitbanter rolling and prevents gaming through self-promotion (tips are disbursed in 0.05BTC increments due to Coinbase API limitation). Okay, so what is this thing?

Technically speaking, it's a Meteor and Coinbase-powered web app designed for speed, simplicity, and random acts of humor. To use my favorite startup metaphor trope: "Bitbanter is the bitcoin-addicted, ambiguously-fathered lovechild orphaned after a tawdry threesome between reddit, McSweenys, and The Onion."

...let's approach this from another angle. Bitbanter is my attempt at a decentralized Comedian's Stone; like a Philosopher's Stone, but instead of transforming base metals into pure gold, the Comedian's Stone turns base humor into pure value ("that's comedy gold, Jerry!"). Consider this diagram:

To me, the Internet has always been about making these arrows more efficient -- reducing barriers, not just for humorists/consumers, but for all creators/consumers -- and Bitbanter is my experiment to that end.

From a more self-motivated perspective: I wanted a place to write satire pseudonymously, to give people some laughs, and to convert some of those laughs into bitcoins...all while promoting the content that generated the most laughs. A platform like that didn't exist, so I made one.

We're living in an era filled with cool shit, like decentralized crypto-currencies, rapid-deployment reactive web frameworks, electric cars, and polar bear fecal transplants. Bitbanter owes its existence to that cool shit. Especially that polar bear fecal transplant, which was a goddamn lifesaver.

How long until you start showing ads?

To me, reducing the barriers between creators/consumers includes telling ads and data collection to bugger off. In addition to being uninspired, those models typically distort the incentives of both content producers and consumers. Though in some important cases, like Google search/local, things can line up nicely for everyone involved. ::Obligatory golf clap to the Google Overlords::

The only data I collect is whatever people voluntarily submit with their articles. And even a user's public bitcoin address is hidden from other users (it's stored on the server). As long as I'm around, Bitbanter will never descend into ads/data territory.

Great, how does it work?

When you first visit Bitbanter, you'll see a list of articles with bitcoin values on the right side. Those bitcoin values are the total amount of tips granted by other users. The more tips, the higher the article. Click on an article's headline to read more. To tip an author for a chuckle (or promote your own work, you cheeky bastard), copy/click on the bitcoin address within the article or click the bitcoin amount to see a totally useful QR code (if you're one of the five people with a mobile bitcoin app). Tip this address using your preferred bitcoin wallet and it will be added to the article's total after confirmation in the blockchain. 80% of your bitcoins go to the author (in 0.04BTC increments -- Coinbase API limitation, hoping it will change in the future) and 20% will go to Bitbanter (suck it, cheeky self-promoters!).

You can submit an article by clicking on the "+" in the header. You're only required to submit a headline and a public bitcoin address. It can be completely anonymous, but you can still receive tips at the public bitcoin address you specify. After submitting an article, Bitbanter will display articles in descending order based on submission date to confirm that your article was added.

Click on the Bitbanter title to change article ordering between "most recent" and "highest tips." Click on "t=" to change the time range of articles submitted (right now you can choose between all articles, last 7 days, and last 30 days). And that's it!

Why bitcoins?

They reduce payment friction, they're transnational, they support micropayments (a penny for a laugh!) and they enable both anonymous content and tipping. If you choose, both Bitbanter's readers and I will never know who you are, but you'll get to write what you want (without revealing your identity, like me) and you can still get credit (through bitcoins and your Twitter handle).

SVG images? Seriously? Why?

Because bitmaps, being not entirely dissimilar to your mother, are fat and slow. With SVGs, people can still have some creative graphics, the site will scale more easily, and I can keep everything running in Meteor's built-in MongoDB database.

They really take away from the simplicity aesthetic, while existing social networks enable a lot of within-network conversations about a link...which is another way of saying I'm lazy and it looked difficult to implement.
